Sustainability Through the Ages

Explore Intel’s journey through time, discovering how our commitment to innovation has shaped a more sustainable future for technology and our planet.

Key Milestones

1968

Intel Founded

Robert Noyce and Gordon Moore rename NM Electronics to Intel Corporation, laying the foundation for decades of innovation.

This set the stage for Intel’s legacy in microprocessors and semiconductor breakthroughs.

1971

First Microprocessor

Intel debuts the 4004, the world’s first commercial microprocessor, igniting a computing revolution.

That chip changed everything—from calculators to personal computers.

1978

8086 Processor

Launch of the 8086, establishing the x86 architecture that drives modern PCs and servers.

The x86 family remains at the heart of most desktops and laptops.

1985

386 Processor

Intel introduces the 386 (32-bit), ushering in new performance capabilities for personal computers.

32-bit computing opened doors for complex software and multitasking.

2006

Peak GHG Emissions

Intel’s highest annual greenhouse-gas emissions in operations. Shortly after, massive investments in abatement & renewables begin.

Sustainability shifted from “nice-to-have” to a core corporate strategy.

2020

RISE Strategy

Intel rolls out RISE (Responsible, Inclusive, Sustainable, Enabling), setting 2030 goals on climate, water, and waste.

RISE provided a roadmap for Intel’s next decade of sustainability leadership.

2022

Net-Zero by 2040

Intel pledges net-zero GHG (Scope 1 & 2) across all global operations by 2040.

An ambitious commitment that spurred industry-wide eco innovations.

2023

99% Renewable Electricity

Intel hits 99% renewable electricity usage worldwide, drastically lowering carbon emissions.

Nearly all power now comes from wind, solar, and other renewables.

2024

Sustainability Summit

Intel hosts its first Sustainability Summit, uniting suppliers, officials, and industry leaders to accelerate Eco-innovations.

A global forum to co-create the next generation of sustainable chip manufacturing.

About This Project

This site was built by Preston Bueche (buechepreston.github.io) to showcase Intel’s sustainability milestones, from the very first microprocessor to net-zero ambitions. Every image and text is styled for high contrast and full accessibility. Feel free to browse the timeline, subscribe for updates, or read the reflections below.

Reflections

In building this site, I used randomness when choosing sample placeholder images for “2006,” “2020,” “2022,” etc. Because the images were not official Intel art, I rated them with a probability distribution—from 0 to 100—to pick the one that best represents each milestone. This exercise helped me think about how AI can “guess” the most likely image a user expects, then refine it based on user feedback. It’s a small example of how generative or probabilistic AI could automatically pick the “best image” for marketing pages.

During my Lululemon interview prep, I learned the importance of open-ended questions, like “How do you keep customers engaged?” In a web-dev context, that taught me to build my website so visitors can easily scroll, click, and explore—rather than just stare at a static page. By adding interactive accordion sections and smooth scrolling, I answered my own interview question: “How can I keep a user engaged for longer than 30 seconds?” The result: a clean design that encourages discovery.

For my LinkedIn post, I wrote about Week 1: “It’s weird to not learn AI first, then apply it.” I realized that, for web-dev, hands-on building (like creating this site) cements what I learn about JavaScript, Bootstrap, and accessibility. By posting my journey with real screenshots, I engaged my network and sparked conversations about how AI and code interconnect—exactly the kind of professional branding I aimed for.
